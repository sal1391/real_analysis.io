Which tool do I use?

Problem says: “prove from the definition”

Use the exact $\varepsilon$ definition. Do scratch work to choose $N$, then write the proof cleanly.

Sequence is trapped between two simpler sequences

Use the Squeeze Theorem.

Sequence is increasing/decreasing and bounded

Use the Monotone Convergence Theorem.

You need a convergent subsequence from a bounded sequence

Use Bolzano-Weierstrass.

You do not know the limit, but terms get close to each other

Try Cauchy. In $\mathbb R$, Cauchy implies convergent.

Try subsequences or limsup/liminf. If two subsequences have different limits, the sequence diverges.

Proof: (-1)^n Diverges

Precise statement: A sequence $\{x_n\}$ converges to $L\in\mathbb R$ if

$$\forall \varepsilon>0,\ \exists N\in\mathbb N\text{ such that }n\ge N\Rightarrow |x_n-L|<\varepsilon.$$
Intuition: You give me any tiny tolerance $\varepsilon$. I must find a starting index $N$ so that every term after $N$ is trapped inside the band $(L-\varepsilon,L+\varepsilon)$.

Template: prove $x_n\to L$ directly

  1. Guess the limit $L$ using algebra, graphing, or intuition.
  2. Scratch work: solve $|x_n-L|<\varepsilon$ for a condition like $n>1/\varepsilon$.
  3. Choose $N$ large enough. Usually $N=\lceil \text{something in }\varepsilon\rceil+1$.
  4. Proof: Let $\varepsilon>0$. Choose that $N$. If $n\ge N$, show the inequality chain ending with $<\varepsilon$.
Example: prove $1/n\to 0$

Scratch work: $|1/n-0|=1/n<\varepsilon$, so we want $n>1/\varepsilon$.

Proof: Let $\varepsilon>0$. Choose $N=\lceil 1/\varepsilon\rceil+1$. Then $N>1/\varepsilon$, so $1/N<\varepsilon$. If $n\ge N$, then

$$\left|\frac1n-0\right|=\frac1n\le \frac1N<\varepsilon.$$

Therefore $1/n\to0$.

Example: prove $\frac{n}{n^2+1}\to 0$

Since $n^2+1>n^2$, we have $0<\frac{n}{n^2+1}<\frac{n}{n^2}=\frac1n$.

Then use the same $N$ as the $1/n$ proof, or use Squeeze Theorem.

Template: prove a sequence diverges by contradiction

  1. Assume the sequence converges to some $L$.
  2. Pick a useful $\varepsilon$, often $\varepsilon=1/2$ or less than half the gap between two repeating values.
  3. Use the definition to force two far-apart terms to both be close to $L$.
  4. Use triangle inequality to get an impossible result.
Common mistake: Saying “it jumps around” is intuition, not a proof. For $(-1)^n$, show two subsequences go to different limits or use the contradiction proof.

In a metric space $(X,d)$, a sequence $\{x_n\}$ converges to $x\in X$ if

$$\forall \varepsilon>0,\ \exists N\in\mathbb N\text{ such that }n\ge N\Rightarrow d(x_n,x)<\varepsilon.$$
Only change: replace $|x_n-x|$ with $d(x_n,x)$. Everything else about the quantifiers is the same.

Problem template

  1. Write down the metric $d(x,y)$.
  2. Plug in $x_n$ and the proposed limit $x$.
  3. Simplify $d(x_n,x)<\varepsilon$.
  4. Choose $N$ from the simplified inequality.
Example: $x_n=1/n$ in the arctan metric

Let $d(x,y)=|\arctan x-\arctan y|$. To prove $1/n\to0$:

$$d(1/n,0)=|\arctan(1/n)-\arctan(0)|=\arctan(1/n).$$

We want $\arctan(1/n)<\varepsilon$. Since arctan is increasing, it is enough to make $1/n<\tan\varepsilon$ for small positive $\varepsilon$.

3. Cauchy sequence

Intro to Cauchy Sequences

Cauchy iff Convergent

Open on YouTube

Cauchy Sequences are Bounded

Open on YouTube

Convergent Sequences are Cauchy

Open on YouTube

In $\mathbb R$: $\{x_n\}$ is Cauchy if

$$\forall \varepsilon>0,\ \exists N\in\mathbb N\text{ such that }n,k\ge N\Rightarrow |x_n-x_k|<\varepsilon.$$

In a metric space $(X,d)$:

$$n,k\ge N\Rightarrow d(x_n,x_k)<\varepsilon.$$
Convergent vs. Cauchy: Convergent means the terms get close to a known limit. Cauchy means the terms get close to each other, even before you know the limit.

Template: prove a sequence is Cauchy directly

  1. Start with $|x_n-x_k|$.
  2. Bound it using expressions that go to $0$ when $n,k$ are large.
  3. Choose $N$ so each part is less than $\varepsilon/2$ or $\varepsilon$.
  4. Conclude $|x_n-x_k|<\varepsilon$ for all $n,k\ge N$.
Example: prove $1/n$ is Cauchy

For $n,k\ge N$:

$$\left|\frac1n-\frac1k\right|\le \left|\frac1n\right|+\left|\frac1k\right|\le \frac1N+\frac1N=\frac2N.$$

Choose $N>2/\varepsilon$. Then $2/N<\varepsilon$.

Template: prove not Cauchy

  1. Pick one $\varepsilon>0$ that will fail, usually based on a repeated gap.
  2. Show that no matter how large $N$ is, you can find $n,k\ge N$ with $|x_n-x_k|\ge\varepsilon$.
Example: $(-1)^n$ is not Cauchy

Take $\varepsilon=1$. For any $N$, choose an even $n\ge N$ and an odd $k\ge N$. Then $x_n=1$ and $x_k=-1$, so $|x_n-x_k|=2\ge1$.

Supremum

$s=\sup A$ means $s$ is the least upper bound. It is the tightest ceiling.

Infimum

$m=\inf A$ means $m$ is the greatest lower bound. It is the tightest floor.

Two-part proof for $s=\sup A$

  1. Show $s$ is an upper bound: for all $a\in A$, $a\le s$.
  2. Show it is the least upper bound: if $u$ is any upper bound, then $s\le u$.

This says: if you go even a tiny bit below the supremum, the set must have some element above that point.

Example: prove $\sup(0,1)=1$

First, $1$ is an upper bound because every $x\in(0,1)$ satisfies $x\le1$.

5. Least Upper Bound Property of $\mathbb R$

Supremum and Infimum

Open on YouTube

Nested Interval Property and Proof

Open on YouTube

Playlist item #9: Completeness/Infimums (may be members only)

Open on YouTube

Precise statement: Every nonempty subset of $\mathbb R$ that is bounded above has a supremum in $\mathbb R$.

Intuition: The real numbers do not have “holes.” If a set has a ceiling, it has a tightest ceiling inside $\mathbb R$.

How it shows up in problems

  1. You have a nonempty set $A\subseteq\mathbb R$.
  2. You show $A$ is bounded above.
  3. Then you are allowed to say $\sup A$ exists.
  4. Use that supremum as the candidate limit, especially in the Monotone Convergence Theorem.
Non-example: In $\mathbb Q$, the set $\{q\in\mathbb Q:q^2<2\}$ is bounded above in $\mathbb Q$, but the natural supremum would be $\sqrt2$, which is not rational. This is why $\mathbb Q$ is not complete.

6. Limit superior and limit inferior

Limit Superior and Limit Inferior Explained

Open on YouTube

Sequence Order Limit Theorem

Open on YouTube

Bounded Set Contains Sequence Converging to Supremum

Open on YouTube

For a bounded sequence $\{x_n\}$, define the tail supremum and tail infimum:

$$a_n=\sup\{x_k:k\ge n\},\qquad b_n=\inf\{x_k:k\ge n\}.$$

Then

$$\limsup_{n\to\infty}x_n=\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n,\qquad \liminf_{n\to\infty}x_n=\lim_{n\to\infty}b_n.$$
Intuition: $\limsup$ is the eventual ceiling. $\liminf$ is the eventual floor. Ignore finite early behavior and ask: what high and low values keep appearing forever?

Fast computation method

  1. Look at the long-term behavior, not the first few terms.
  2. Find the highest value or subsequential limit that keeps happening forever. That is the limsup.
  3. Find the lowest value or subsequential limit that keeps happening forever. That is the liminf.
  4. If they are equal, the sequence converges to that common value.
Example: $x_n=(-1)^n$

The sequence keeps hitting $1$ and $-1$ forever. So:

$$\limsup x_n=1,\qquad \liminf x_n=-1.$$
Example: $x_n=1/n$

The tails are positive and shrink toward $0$. The largest value in the $n$-tail is $1/n$, and the infimum of every tail is $0$.

$$\limsup x_n=0,\qquad \liminf x_n=0.$$
Example: periodic sequence $0,1,2,0,1,2,\ldots$

Each tail still contains $0,1,2$ forever, so:

$$\limsup x_n=2,\qquad \liminf x_n=0.$$

7. Metric space and metric/distance function

Triangle Inequality

Reverse Triangle Inequality

Open on YouTube

Neighborhoods

Open on YouTube

A metric space is a set $X$ with a distance function $d:X\times X\to\mathbb R$ satisfying, for all $x,y,z\in X$:

Metric ruleMeaning
Positive definiteness$d(x,y)\ge0$ and $d(x,y)=0$ iff $x=y$.
Symmetry$d(x,y)=d(y,x)$.
Triangle inequality$d(x,z)\le d(x,y)+d(y,z)$.

Template: prove something is a metric

  1. Check distances are never negative.
  2. Check distance is $0$ exactly when the two points are the same.
  3. Check symmetry by swapping $x$ and $y$.
  4. Prove triangle inequality. This is usually the hardest part.

Common examples

Standard metric on $\mathbb R$

$d(x,y)=|x-y|$.

Discrete metric

$d(x,y)=0$ if $x=y$ and $1$ if $x\ne y$.

Euclidean metric

$d(x,y)=\sqrt{(x_1-y_1)^2+\cdots+(x_n-y_n)^2}$.

Arctan metric

$d(x,y)=|\arctan x-\arctan y|$.

8. Completeness

Cauchy iff Convergent

Open on YouTube

Nested Interval Property

Open on YouTube

Bolzano-Weierstrass via Nested Intervals

Open on YouTube

A metric space $(X,d)$ is complete if every Cauchy sequence in $X$ converges to a point in $X$.

$$(X,d)\text{ complete}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \text{every Cauchy sequence in }X\text{ has its limit inside }X.$$
Key idea: Completeness means no missing limit points for Cauchy sequences.

How to prove not complete

  1. Find a sequence inside the space.
  2. Show it is Cauchy.
  3. Show its natural limit is not inside the space.
  4. Conclude the space is not complete.
Example: $((0,1),|\cdot|)$ is not complete

The sequence $x_n=1/n$ is in $(0,1)$ for $n\ge2$ and is Cauchy, but it converges to $0$, and $0\notin(0,1)$. So $(0,1)$ is not complete.

Example: $([0,1],|\cdot|)$ is complete

Any Cauchy sequence in $[0,1]$ is Cauchy in $\mathbb R$, so it converges to some real number. Since $[0,1]$ is closed, the limit remains in $[0,1]$.

9. Monotone Convergence Theorem

What are Monotone Sequences?

Open on YouTube

Detailed Proof of MCT

Open on YouTube

Using MCT

Open on YouTube

Precise statement: A monotone sequence is bounded if and only if it is convergent. More specifically:

$$\text{If }x_n\text{ is increasing and bounded, then }\lim x_n=\sup\{x_n:n\in\mathbb N\}.$$
$$\text{If }x_n\text{ is decreasing and bounded, then }\lim x_n=\inf\{x_n:n\in\mathbb N\}.$$

Template: use MCT

  1. Show monotone: prove $x_n\le x_{n+1}$ or $x_n\ge x_{n+1}$.
  2. Show bounded: find an upper bound if increasing, or lower bound if decreasing.
  3. State: by MCT, the sequence converges.
  4. If asked for the limit, identify the supremum or infimum.
Common mistake: “Looks increasing” is not enough. You must show it algebraically, usually by comparing $x_{n+1}-x_n$ or $x_{n+1}/x_n$.

10. Squeeze Theorem

Proof: Sequence Squeeze Theorem

Open on YouTube

Example: 1/sqrt(n) -> 0

Open on YouTube

Sequence Order Limit Theorem

Open on YouTube

Precise statement: If $a_n\le x_n\le b_n$ for all sufficiently large $n$, and

$$a_n\to L\quad\text{and}\quad b_n\to L,$$

then $x_n\to L$.

Template: use Squeeze

  1. Find a lower bound $a_n$ and upper bound $b_n$ for $x_n$.
  2. Show both bounding sequences converge to the same limit $L$.
  3. Conclude $x_n\to L$ by Squeeze Theorem.
Example: $1/\sqrt n\to0$

A clean direct proof uses:

$$\frac1{\sqrt n}<\varepsilon\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad n>\frac1{\varepsilon^2}.$$

Choose $N>1/\varepsilon^2$.

Example: $\frac{\sin n}{n}\to0$

Since $-1\le\sin n\le1$,

$$-\frac1n\le \frac{\sin n}{n}\le \frac1n.$$

Both $-1/n$ and $1/n$ converge to $0$, so $\sin n/n\to0$.

11. Ratio Test for convergence of sequences

Sequence Limit Laws

Open on YouTube

Intro to Infinite Series

Open on YouTube

Full playlist search: ratio/series

Open on YouTube

Your course version: Let $x_n\ne0$ eventually and suppose

$$L=\lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{x_{n+1}{x_n}\right|$$

exists. Then:

  • If $L<1$, then $x_n\to0$.
  • If $L>1$, then $\{x_n\}$ is unbounded, hence diverges.
  • If $L=1$, the test is inconclusive.
Playlist note: I did not find an exact “Ratio Test for sequences” video in this playlist. The closest supports are the sequence limit laws and the series section. Use your Day 5 notes as the main source for this theorem.

Template: use the Ratio Test

  1. Compute $\left|x_{n+1}/x_n\right|$.
  2. Simplify until it is easy to take the limit.
  3. If the limit is $<1$, conclude $x_n\to0$.
  4. If the limit is $>1$, conclude unbounded/divergent.
  5. If the limit is $1$, switch methods.
Example: $x_n=2^n/n!$
$$\left|\frac{x_{n+1}{x_n}\right|=\frac{2^{n+1}{(n+1)!}\cdot\frac{n!}{2^n}=\frac2{n+1}\to0<1.$$

Therefore $2^n/n!\to0$.

Example: $x_n=2^n$
$$\left|\frac{x_{n+1}{x_n}\right|=\frac{2^{n+1}{2^n}=2>1.$$

So $2^n$ is unbounded and diverges.

12. Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem

Intro to Subsequences

Monotone Subsequence Theorem

Open on YouTube

Short Proof of Bolzano-Weierstrass

Open on YouTube

Bolzano-Weierstrass via Nested Intervals

Open on YouTube

Sequence version: Every bounded sequence of real numbers has a convergent subsequence.

Intuition: A bounded sequence cannot escape to infinity. Even if it jumps around, some subsequence must settle down.

How to use it

  1. Show the sequence is bounded.
  2. State: by Bolzano-Weierstrass, there exists a convergent subsequence.
  3. If needed, name it $x_{n_k}$ and call its limit $L$.

How it helps prove divergence

If you can find two subsequences with different limits, the original sequence cannot converge.

13. How to prove a sequence converges

This is the main exam skill. The trick is choosing the shortest valid method.

Problem typeBest methodProof sentence to use
Explicit formula like $1/n$, $n/(n^2+1)$Direct $\varepsilon$ proof or Squeeze“Let $\varepsilon>0$. Choose $N=\cdots$.”
Trapped expression like $\sin n/n$Squeeze“Since $a_n\le x_n\le b_n$ and both bounds go to $L$, by Squeeze…”
Recursive or monotone-lookingMCT“The sequence is monotone and bounded, so it converges by MCT.”
Factorials/exponentialsRatio Test“The ratio tends to $L<1$, so $x_n\to0$.”

Reusable proof skeleton: direct convergence

Proof. Let ε > 0. Choose N = ______. Then for every n ≥ N, |x_n - L| = ______ ≤ ______ < ε. Therefore x_n → L.

Reusable proof skeleton: theorem-based

Proof. We verify the assumptions of [theorem]. First, ______. Second, ______. Therefore, by [theorem], the sequence converges.

14. How to compute limsup and liminf

Limsup/Liminf Explained

Open on YouTube

Subsequences

Open on YouTube

Bolzano-Weierstrass

Open on YouTube

Method A: tail sup/inf method

  1. Write the $n$-tail: $\{x_k:k\ge n\}$.
  2. Find $a_n=\sup$ of that tail.
  3. Find $b_n=\inf$ of that tail.
  4. Take $\lim a_n$ and $\lim b_n$.

Method B: subsequential-limit method

  1. Find all values the sequence approaches along subsequences.
  2. The largest subsequential limit is $\limsup$.
  3. The smallest subsequential limit is $\liminf$.
SequenceBehavior$\limsup$$\liminf$
$1/n$decreases to $0$$0$$0$
$(-1)^n$alternates $-1,1$$1$$-1$
$(-1)^n+1/n$even terms near $1$, odd terms near $-1$$1$$-1$
$0,1,2,0,1,2,\ldots$periodic$2$$0$
$x_n=1$ if $n$ is a square, $0$ otherwiseboth $1$ and $0$ happen forever$1$$0$
Convergence test: For a bounded sequence, $x_n$ converges if and only if $\limsup x_n=\liminf x_n$. If equal, the common value is the limit.
Practice

Practice set with answers

1. Prove $\frac{3n+1}{n+2}\to3$.

Compute:

$$\left|\frac{3n+1}{n+2}-3\right|=\left|\frac{3n+1-3n-6}{n+2}\right|=\frac5{n+2}.$$

Choose $N>5/\varepsilon$. Then for $n\ge N$, $5/(n+2)<5/n\le5/N<\varepsilon$.

2. Show $\sin n/n\to0$.

Use Squeeze: $-1/n\le \sin n/n\le1/n$. Both bounds go to $0$.

3. Find $\limsup$ and $\liminf$ of $x_n=(-1)^n+1/n$.

Even terms go to $1$, odd terms go to $-1$. Therefore $\limsup x_n=1$ and $\liminf x_n=-1$.

4. Prove $2^n/n!\to0$.

Use Ratio Test:

$$\left|\frac{x_{n+1}{x_n}\right|=\frac2{n+1}\to0<1.$$

Therefore $x_n\to0$.

5. Is $((0,1),|\cdot|)$ complete?

No. The sequence $1/n$ is Cauchy but converges to $0$, which is not in $(0,1)$.

6. State Bolzano-Weierstrass and give an example.

Every bounded sequence in $\mathbb R$ has a convergent subsequence. Example: $(-1)^n$ is bounded and has subsequences constantly $1$ and constantly $-1$, both convergent.
